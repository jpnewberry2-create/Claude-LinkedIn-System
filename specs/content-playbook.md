# LinkedIn High-Performing Content — Playbook

**Purpose.** Actionable principles for producing the highest-performing LinkedIn content, written to instruct both Jamie and LLMs. Four research areas: post body structure, posting times (UK), profile optimisation, reference accounts. All data 2026 unless noted.

**How to use this.** Three downstream outputs draw from here: the LLM content brief (the prompt that instructs Claude when drafting posts), the pre-publish checklist (QA before posting), and the brand guidelines update. Sections below are tagged for which output they feed.

## 1. Post body structure (the caption beneath the carousel)

The caption is a second scroll-stop, separate from the cover slide. The carousel wins the swipe; the caption wins the "see more" click. Both are first-hour engagement inputs.

### The rule that overrides everything

The first two lines decide the post's reach. They are the only guaranteed real estate before LinkedIn truncates with "see more." Roughly 65% of users decide whether to expand based on the opening line alone, and the hook determines around 80% of a post's performance: the same body with a stronger hook gets 2–5× the engagement. If the first two lines fail, nothing after them matters.

### The cutoff is brutal and mobile-first

- Mobile shows roughly **200 characters** (~2–3 lines) before "see more." Desktop shows ~300. Over 60% of usage is mobile, so design for ~200.
- Think in **lines, not characters**. One sentence then a line break uses ~70 characters and shows a clean hook. One 200-character run-on shows a wall and gets scrolled.
- **The phone test (non-negotiable):** before posting, view the draft on your own phone in portrait. If the hook cuts off mid-word or reads as a paragraph, rewrite it. This single habit lifts see-more clicks more than any other change.

### The three-part structure

Every post: **Hook → Body → CTA.**

1. **Hook (lines 1–2).** A bold claim, surprising stat, contrarian opinion, or relatable observation. Appears above the fold. Carries 80% of the weight.
2. **Body.** The substance: a story, a framework, a list, or an argument. Short paragraphs, generous line breaks, one to two sentences per paragraph. Walls of text get scrolled regardless of quality.
3. **CTA (last 1–2 lines).** One directive. A specific question that invites a comment.

A slightly expanded version for longer posts: Hook (1–2) → Context/setup (3–5 lines) → Value delivery (the story/framework/data that pays off the hook) → Takeaway + CTA. The point is that readers who click past the hook stay to the end, which signals quality to the algorithm.

### Caption length for a carousel specifically

**Carousel/document captions: 300–500 characters.** The carousel carries the depth; the caption frames it and drives the click. Don't write a 2,000-character essay under a carousel; the slides are the content.

Reference points for other formats:
- Standard text/image post sweet spot: ~1,800–2,100 characters when the idea has depth.
- Quick insight / busy-exec scan: under 300 characters.
- Pure question post: 100–300 characters. Shorter question = more answers.
- Breaking-news commentary: 400–700 characters. Speed beats depth when news is fresh.
- Choose length by the depth of the idea, not the maximum allowed.

### Formatting mechanics

- **Line breaks and white space** are the highest-leverage formatting lever. A blank line immediately after the hook creates a visual pause that pulls the "see more" click.
- **Unicode bold/italic** for the hook or key phrases (LinkedIn's composer has no native bold). Use sparingly; don't bold half the post.
- **Symbols** (→, ✓, •) create structure cheaply and render on both desktop and mobile.
- **List format:** hook line, blank line, then a numbered or bulleted list, each item one to two lines max.
- **No external links in the body.** ~60% reach penalty; link-in-first-comment is also now mildly penalised. Native value first, link as afterthought in comments.
- **Hashtags:** 3–5 maximum, niche not broad.
- **Tag 2–3 people maximum**, only those who genuinely add to the conversation.

### Hook formulas (the engine)

Ranked by measured performance. **Curiosity-gap and contrarian hooks are the top two**, at ~6.8% and ~6.2% engagement, and together they outperform all other types by ~2.3×. **Hooks under 10 words beat longer hooks by ~40%.** Keep every hook under ~12 words and drop straight into tension, no context preamble.

The three-part hook construction: **specific detail + emotional trigger + open loop.** If the line could appear in a press release, rewrite it. If cutting the first sentence makes the post better, cut it.

| Formula | Pattern | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Curiosity gap** | "I stopped X for 30 days. The result surprised me." | Opens a loop only a click can close. Top performer. |
| **Contrarian / "X is dead"** | "Cold outreach is dead. Here's what works in 2026." | Challenges consensus, generates debate (= comments). |
| **Specific result** | "This one habit tripled my output. Zero experience needed." | Desirable outcome + barrier removed. |
| **The exact asset** | "I'll show you the exact template that converted at 45%." | Promises a tangible tool with proof. |
| **Personal stakes** | "Your headline is costing you opportunities every single day." | Ongoing, urgent, personal loss. |
| **Numbered list** | "7 mistakes silently killing your reach." | Reader knows exactly what they're getting. |
| **Uncomfortable truth** | "Most LinkedIn advice is wrong. I can prove it." | Validates a suspicion, promises proof. |

Rule on bold claims: only use them when the body backs them up. An unsupported bold claim reads as clickbait and burns credibility. This matters doubly given the "honest, not cynical" pillar.

**Validate hooks for free:** drop a candidate hook as the opening line of a comment on a popular post in the niche. If it gets replies, it's validated. Keep a swipe file: screenshot every post that stops your own scroll before line two.

> **Feeds:** LLM brief (hook formulas, structure, length, formatting rules), pre-publish checklist (phone test, fold check, link check, hashtag count), guidelines (the hook-carries-80% principle).

## 2. Posting times — UK audience

Timing matters because the algorithm evaluates engagement velocity in the first 60–90 minutes. Posting when the UK audience is active gives a head start; optimal timing can lift impressions 30–50% versus off-peak. Posts circulate for 24–48 hours, so the first few hours are decisive.

### The core window

- **Best days: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday.** Consensus across Buffer (4.8M posts), Sprout Social (2.7B engagements), and UK-specific analyses. **Wednesday is the single strongest day.**
- **Worst days: Saturday and Sunday.** Saturday is worst.
- **Monday is weaker than most guides claim.** Avoid posting before 9 AM Monday. Friday afternoons are largely wasted for professional content.

### UK times (set schedule to GMT, or BST in summer)

- **Primary (morning):** 8–11 AM. UK B2B engagement is strongest here. **Tuesday 8 AM and Wednesday 12 PM** consistently return the highest UK B2B engagement.
- **Three UK daily peaks** for B2B: **8–9 AM** (commute), **12–1 PM** (lunch), **5–6 PM** (end of day).
- **The 2026 shift:** late afternoon and evening (3–10 PM) are gaining, as mobile usage blurs work/life boundaries. Sprout's UK all-platform peak is Thursday 4–11 PM. Treat evening as a secondary test slot, not the default.

### Tech-audience nuance (closest to Jamie's positioning)

Tech audiences engage more evenly across the day, but **Tuesday 9–11 AM is the strongest single window** — many UK tech companies run a 9 AM standup, freeing people for LinkedIn straight after. The 8–10 AM Tue–Thu window also suits the consultant/advisory audience.

### Format-by-time

- **Carousels and long thought-leadership:** post **earlier in the day** (morning), when readers have time to engage with depth.
- **Short posts and polls:** mid-morning is fine.
- **Breaking-news commentary:** speed beats timing; post when the news is fresh.

### UK bank holidays (suppress engagement)

- Avoid high-priority posts around bank holidays. For Good Friday (3 April 2026), avoid ~2–7 April for important posts.
- The week *after* a bank holiday sees elevated engagement as people catch up. **Tuesday of the return week is often stronger than a normal Tuesday.**

### Practical default

**Tue/Wed/Thu, publish ~9 AM GMT, then stay on the platform for the first hour to reply to comments.** Wednesday for the most important post of the week. Reserve one evening slot (Tue or Thu, ~5–6 PM) for a test once a cadence is established.

> **Feeds:** guidelines (the schedule), content calendar, pre-publish checklist (right day/time + first-hour-reply commitment).

## 3. Profile optimisation

**Critical framing first — resolve before applying any of this.** Almost all profile-optimisation advice online is written for lead-gen or job-hunting: "who you help + outcome + CTA to book a call," problem-agitation-solution About sections, Calendly links in Featured. **This collides head-on with the locked master rule: profile is credibility, not sales.** Jamie's profile is KPMG-anchored credibility furniture; the original-thinker brand lives in content. So the mechanics below are adopted *selectively*: take the SEO/searchability and clarity mechanics, drop the salesy conversion framing.

### Why the profile still matters

People decide in 3–6 seconds whether you're credible and relevant. LinkedIn is also a keyword-indexed database: it now uses AI semantic search (depth matters, not just keyword matches), and consistent activity — even one post a week — boosts profile visibility.

### Headline (220 chars; highest-impact field)

Appears in every search result, notification, and comment. **For Jamie (credibility, not sales):** lead with the KPMG-anchored role for institutional credibility, then signal the content territory (building, AI, emerging tech) without a salesy "I help X do Y" promise. The headline should make a curious reader understand the role and the point of view, not pitch a service. Keep keywords relevant for searchability; avoid "seeking opportunities" or vague passion statements.

### About section (2,600 chars; first ~300 visible)

First ~300 characters appear in search previews and before "see more," so they carry the most weight — front-load the terms a reader would actually search. Write first-person. **For Jamie:** keep the first-person voice and front-loaded clarity, but reframe from sales-funnel to credibility-and-POV. Opening lines state what Jamie thinks about and builds, not a client promise. Apply the voice rules (UK English, no banned words, lead with the substance).

### Featured section (digital portfolio, top of profile)

**For Jamie:** the cleanest place to showcase the *content* without breaking the credibility rule — pin best-performing carousels or thought pieces here. A proof gallery of the original-thinker brand, not a sales shelf. No Calendly, no lead magnets.

### Banner (1584×396)

**For Jamie:** already on the build list (rebuild replacing the old wave cover, retain the KPMG subtitle). Use it for the "bringing ideas to life" positioning and the visual identity, with the KPMG subtitle carrying institutional credibility. Brand-system colours and type, not a sales banner.

### Secondary SEO mechanics (adopt freely — these are neutral)

- **Experience titles:** make vague titles specific and searchable without inflating them.
- **Skills:** a secondary keyword index; the top 3 pinned skills carry the most weight.
- **Profile photo:** a clear, well-lit headshot materially lifts profile views.
- **Recommendations:** specific, outcome-named recommendations beat generic praise.
- **Certifications / recent skill activity:** profiles with recent skill certifications get ~40% more search appearances.

> **Feeds:** guidelines (profile section, with the credibility-not-sales reframe explicit), the banner rebuild task, a one-time profile-rewrite task.

## 4. Reference accounts and what to study

**Honest caveat.** Live engagement metrics and design teardowns of specific named profiles aren't reliably retrievable via search, and public "top creator" lists skew to the mega tier whose playbook doesn't transfer to a personal brand built from a smaller base. So this is **a curation principle plus named starting points to study manually**, not a verified benchmark set. The highest-value action is the swipe-file habit.

### The curation principle (what separates signal from noise)

Maps onto the four personality pillars:

- **Builders over commentators.** Prioritise people who ship products, run engineering teams, or build companies. (Maps to *Builder-biased.*)
- **"How," not "Wow."** Seek creators who explain the mechanics, not demo videos captioned "Mind-blowing!" (Maps to *Curious, not credulous* and *Plain-spoken.*)
- **Operator credibility.** The accounts worth studying say things work because they tested them. (Maps to *Honest, not cynical.*)

### Named accounts to study manually

**Builder/operator voices (closest to the positioning):** Lenny Rachitsky (operator credibility, testing-led claims); Brian de Haaff / Aha! (owning a niche through consistent expertise); Rand Fishkin / SparkToro (data/research carousels, contrarian-but-evidenced); Ruben Hassid (content mechanics, the builder-who-writes model).

**Technical-but-accessible AI voices (News & Insights pillar):** Andrej Karpathy, Yann LeCun (credible on AI without hype); Cassie Kozyrkov (plain-spoken explanation); Bernard Marr (clear summaries — study format not substance, leans commentator).

**Personal-story carousel craft:** Sophia Amoruso (humanising a brand through story, the narrative-arc carousel).

### What "creative not corporate" looks like in the wild

- **One stark contrast per before/after carousel** — a single transformation, visualised simply. Visual punch over completeness.
- **Headshot, background removed, placed cleanly on a slide** builds parasocial rapport — far better than stock "handshake" photos.
- **Pattern interrupt on slide 2** — change background colour or drop a striking stat to re-engage after the cover.
- **Personal-story and contrarian formats out-engage tip-listicles.**

### The real action

Build a swipe file. Screenshot every LinkedIn post that stops the scroll before line two or makes you want to swipe. After a few months that collection, drawn from the real target audience, is worth more than any external list.

> **Feeds:** guidelines (curation principle as the content-quality filter), a standing swipe-file habit, design reference for the template set.

## Consolidated principles — the short list

**Hook (first 2 lines):**
1. Under ~12 words, ideally under 10. Drop straight into tension, no preamble.
2. Curiosity gap or contrarian by default.
3. Specific detail + emotional trigger + open loop. Never press-release language.
4. Bold claims only if the body proves them.
5. Phone test: view on mobile portrait; if it cuts mid-word or reads as a wall, rewrite.

**Body:**
6. Hook → Body → CTA. Short paragraphs, 1–2 sentences each, generous line breaks.
7. Carousel caption: 300–500 characters. The slides carry the depth.
8. No external links in body. 3–5 niche hashtags max. Tag 2–3 people max.
9. One first-person stake or genuine opinion per post, or it reads corporate.

**CTA:**
10. One directive only. A specific question asking for an *experience*, not an opinion. No yes/no, no "what do you think?"
11. Reply to every comment within the first 2 hours (+30% engagement; comments weighted ~15× likes).

**Timing:**
12. Tue/Wed/Thu, ~9 AM GMT (Wednesday for the week's key post). Carousels in the morning. Stay on-platform the first hour. Avoid weekends, Monday AM, Friday PM, and bank-holiday weeks.

**Format choice:**
13. Carousel for depth/reach; short text post for voice/personality. A sustainable rhythm is ~2 carousels + 2 short posts per week, capped at 3–5 posts weekly total.

**Profile:**
14. Credibility, not sales. Adopt SEO/clarity mechanics; drop the lead-gen framing.

**Voice (always):**
15. UK English. Lead with the answer. No banned words. No em dashes. Warm, direct, dry where it fits. Second gear (contrarian) used rarely and only when data-backed.
